# A Reflection On What Makes a Good Yu-Gi-Oh Duel

## The Problems

I recently was re-watching Yu-Gi-Oh Zexal, specifically the duel between Yuma and Vetrix. I was enjoying the duel, until I start to actually think about the final turn of the duel and how much of an ass-pull it was to allow Yuma to win.

Setting the stage:
- Yuma has 50 life points left and Vetrix activated an effect that would deal 500 damage at the end of Yuma's turn.
- Yuma is in his Main Phase 2, so he can no longer declare an attack.
- Even if he could, Vetrix's monster has an effect that can destroy any monster.
- And Even if he couldn't use that effect, with 3800 LP and a monster with 3100 attack points, Yuma would need a monster with 6900 ATK points to win before his turn ends.

So what does Yuma do, why he summons a monster that has not been seen to this point, and will has not been seen since with the EXACT three effects needed to win.

- Allows Yuma to attack with it during his Main Phase 2
- Can negate a monster's effect, to stop Vetrix's monster.
- While also halving that monster's attack to 1550.

And with this monster having 5500 Atk, it is able to defeat Vetrix in one fell swoop.

Needless to say, I'm not a fan of this duel. I don't like when duels are won with some hyper specific "write yourself out of corner" card, and much prefer when the win has some degree of skill behind it. Now I could go on and talk about more bad writing in Yu-Gi-Oh duels, but I'm instead gonna be more positive, and instead talk about duels that I actually really enjoy, and thought were written/choreographed well.

## What Make a Duel Well Written

Before I discuss some of the duels I like, I want to briefly go over what are some aspects that I like/appreciate in duel writing, just so we are on the similar page.

### Simple Card Effects

One of the best ways to avoid "write yourself out of corner" cards, is to have cards with simple effects. The simpler the effect, the more believable that the card just happens to exist in a world of card games. It overall just helps with immersion.

A mild corollary to this, is that I like when the players play good cards. This may seem like a weird aspect, but I find that when characters play cards that no reasonable person would put in their deck, but happen to save them in a narrow context, I find that is just as contrived.

### Precedence and a Consistent Deck

Quite simply, the more a card is used by a character, the more it is believable that the card is just something that happens to be part of their deck. To a certain extent, this follows from a previous point. This is because the more reused the card is, the more likely it is a simple and widely applicable card, and not a writing contrivance.

### Intentionality when Deck Building

This is probably the least explored aspect of Yu-Gi-Oh duels, but I greatly appreciate when players make a specific strategy to counter a specific opponent. This is an important part of the real game, and I think it's a great way to demonstrate an underrepresented skill in the anime. It also greatly helps in preventing powerful plays from seeming contrived, by framing them as intentional strategies.

### Outplaying

Ultimately, the common theme here is preventing games from feeling contrived, and the common thing of all of these points is that I want to feel like the winner outplayed the loser, instead of them just happening to draw the "I win" card. Now I'm not saying that a good duel has to have all of these aspects. I just find these are tools which make duels more interesting to watch.
